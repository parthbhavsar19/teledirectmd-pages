Cellulitis (Skin Infection): A Physician's Complete Guide

Symptoms, causes, MRSA coverage, and when to seek care — based on IDSA guidelines and explained by a board-certified physician.

Medically reviewed by Parth Bhavsar, MD
Last reviewed: March 2026
15 min read

Key Takeaways

  • Cellulitis is a bacterial infection of the skin and underlying soft tissue, caused most often by Staphylococcus aureus or Group A Streptococcus.
  • The IDSA 2014 skin and soft tissue infection guidelines recommend cephalexin or dicloxacillin as first-line oral treatment for non-purulent cellulitis without MRSA risk factors.[1]
  • When MRSA is suspected — prior MRSA infection, penetrating trauma, injection drug use, or systemic signs — TMP-SMX or doxycycline provides appropriate oral coverage.[1]
  • Marking the border of redness at the time of diagnosis is the most practical way to monitor treatment response.
  • Tinea pedis (athlete's foot) is the single most modifiable risk factor for recurrent lower extremity cellulitis and should always be treated.[3]
  • Go to the emergency department for rapidly spreading redness, fever above 101°F (38.3°C), periorbital involvement, or any cellulitis in a patient who is immunocompromised.

Every week in my practice, I see patients who waited too long with cellulitis. They assumed the redness on their leg was an allergic reaction, a bug bite, or something that would settle down on its own. By the time they came in, the infection had doubled in size and, in some cases, was already causing fever. Cellulitis moves fast. The good news is that when caught early and treated correctly, it almost always responds well to oral antibiotics.

Skin and soft tissue infections account for more than 14 million outpatient visits annually in the United States, with abscess and cellulitis being the most frequent diagnoses within that category.[7] Despite how often we see them, cellulitis is also one of the most misdiagnosed conditions in emergency medicine — studies suggest that up to 30% of suspected cellulitis cases are actually something else entirely, most commonly lipodermatosclerosis or venous stasis dermatitis.[6] Getting the diagnosis right matters because the wrong treatment wastes time and delays care for the real problem.

This guide covers everything I discuss with patients in the exam room: what cellulitis is, how to recognize it, how to tell it apart from other conditions that look similar, how we treat it based on current IDSA guidelines, and — critically — how to keep it from coming back.

What Is Cellulitis?

Cellulitis is a bacterial infection of the dermis (the deep layer of skin) and the subcutaneous tissues just beneath it. It is not a surface infection. That distinction is important because it explains both why it looks the way it does and why topical antibiotic creams have no meaningful role in treating it.

When bacteria breach the skin's outer barrier, they trigger an acute inflammatory response in the deeper tissue layers. Your immune system sends white blood cells to the area, producing the four classic signs: redness, warmth, swelling, and pain. The bacteria also release toxins that damage tissue, which is why prompt treatment prevents the infection from burrowing deeper into muscle or bone.

Cellulitis most commonly affects the lower legs, though it can occur anywhere on the body. In adults, roughly 70–80% of cases involve the lower extremity.[6] The face, periorbital area, and hands are other sites that warrant particular attention, given the anatomical structures at risk if those infections spread.

Symptoms

The typical presentation of cellulitis follows a recognizable pattern. Most patients describe noticing redness that was small at first and then grew over 12–24 hours. By the time they're in my office, the area is warm to the touch, tender, and swollen — and often they can trace where the edge of the redness was yesterday compared to where it is today.

The Core Signs

  • Redness (erythema): The skin appears red or pink, often with poorly defined borders that merge gradually into normal skin. Unlike erysipelas — a more superficial infection — classic cellulitis does not have a sharp, raised border.
  • Warmth: The infected skin is noticeably warmer than surrounding tissue when you press the back of your hand to it.
  • Swelling (edema): The area feels puffy or tight. In the lower leg, swelling may cause the skin to become tense and shiny.
  • Pain or tenderness: The skin hurts to touch. Severe pain out of proportion to what you can see on the surface is a red flag for a deeper, more serious infection like necrotizing fasciitis.
  • Spreading border: Active cellulitis spreads outward over hours to days. A border that is moving is still active infection, even if you are already on antibiotics. Treatment works when the border stops advancing — typically within 24–48 hours of starting the right antibiotic.

Systemic Symptoms

Fever, chills, fatigue, and body aches indicate the infection has triggered a systemic response. Their presence shifts the classification from mild to moderate or severe in the IDSA framework and typically means the infection has moved beyond simple outpatient management.[1] Some patients also develop enlarged, tender lymph nodes near the affected area (regional lymphadenopathy) and red streaking along the skin that tracks toward lymph nodes (lymphangitis). Both are signs that the immune system is working hard and that the infection is moving — see a physician the same day.

Causes

Cellulitis is almost always caused by skin bacteria that get underneath the surface. Two organisms account for the overwhelming majority of cases.

Staphylococcus aureus — including both methicillin-sensitive (MSSA) and methicillin-resistant (MRSA) strains — is the most frequently implicated pathogen in purulent skin and soft tissue infections. In non-purulent cellulitis (no pus, no abscess), beta-hemolytic streptococci — particularly Group A Streptococcus (S. pyogenes) — are the primary cause.[1] This distinction matters because it drives antibiotic selection: Streptococcus is reliably sensitive to beta-lactam antibiotics, while MRSA requires different coverage entirely.

How Bacteria Enter the Skin

Bacteria need a way in. They do not penetrate intact, healthy skin. The most common entry points are:

  • Tinea pedis (athlete's foot): The macerated, cracked skin between the toes is the leading portal of entry for lower extremity cellulitis. Many patients have no idea they have a fungal infection until I examine their feet.
  • Minor cuts, abrasions, and insect bites: Small breaks are sufficient. Scratching a mosquito bite, a paper cut, or a minor graze can introduce bacteria.
  • Leg ulcers and venous stasis wounds: Chronic wounds are almost universally colonized with bacteria and provide direct access to deeper tissue.
  • Surgical wounds and IV sites: Any procedure that disrupts the skin creates a potential entry point.
  • Folliculitis: Infected hair follicles can serve as seeding sites for spreading infection.
  • Injection drug use: Skin popping and IV drug use are associated with higher rates of MRSA and polymicrobial infection.
A Note on Blood Cultures

The IDSA 2014 guidelines do not recommend routine blood cultures for typical outpatient cellulitis because yield is very low — positive in fewer than 2% of cases.[1] Blood cultures are reserved for patients with systemic signs of infection (fever, elevated heart rate, low blood pressure), immunocompromised patients, and those requiring hospitalization. Routine wound swabs of intact cellulitis are similarly unhelpful; the surface bacteria do not reflect what is causing the deep infection.

Risk Factors

Cellulitis is not random. Certain conditions consistently appear in the background of patients who develop it — especially those who get it repeatedly. Understanding these factors is as important as treating the acute infection, because recurrence rates are high when underlying issues go unaddressed.

  • Diabetes mellitus: Elevated blood glucose impairs white blood cell function and slows the immune response. Diabetic patients also develop peripheral neuropathy, meaning minor foot injuries go unnoticed and unaddressed for longer. Skin infections in diabetics can also progress faster and may involve unusual organisms.[3]
  • Lymphedema: Lymphatic drainage removes fluid and carries immune cells to sites of infection. When lymphatic channels are damaged — from prior surgery, cancer treatment, trauma, or filariasis — fluid pools in the tissue, creating a protein-rich environment ideal for bacterial growth. Patients with lymphedema can develop cellulitis from bacteria with no obvious entry point.
  • Obesity: Excess adipose tissue impairs lymphatic drainage in the legs and creates skin folds that trap moisture and reduce local immune surveillance. Obesity is independently associated with both developing and having recurrent cellulitis.[3]
  • Venous insufficiency: Chronic venous disease causes tissue congestion, hypoxia, and chronic skin breakdown that makes the lower legs particularly susceptible to infection.
  • Prior cellulitis: Having one episode of cellulitis is one of the strongest predictors of future episodes. Tissue architecture changes after infection, lymphatic channels may be damaged, and underlying risk factors persist if not actively managed. Prior cellulitis raises the incidence rate substantially — one large cohort study found recurrence-related incidence nearly fivefold higher than in those without prior infection.[7]
  • Tinea pedis: Fungal infection of the feet — particularly between the toes — produces chronic skin fissuring that serves as a persistent bacterial entry point. This is one of the most treatable risk factors and arguably the most overlooked. I examine every cellulitis patient's feet, and a meaningful number have undiagnosed tinea pedis.
  • Immunosuppression: HIV, corticosteroid therapy, chemotherapy, solid organ transplant recipients, and patients receiving biologics all have impaired defenses and may develop atypical or rapidly progressive infections.

Diagnosis

Cellulitis is a clinical diagnosis. There is no blood test or imaging study that definitively confirms it — the diagnosis is made by examining the skin and fitting the picture to the overall clinical context. This is both straightforward and, in practice, frequently wrong.

What I Look For

The characteristic triad is redness, warmth, and tenderness in a patient with a plausible entry point or recognized risk factors. I pay particular attention to:

  • Whether the borders are defined or diffuse — sharp-raised borders suggest erysipelas, not typical cellulitis
  • The presence of fluctuance (a soft, movable center indicating pus) — this means abscess, not pure cellulitis
  • Whether there is a central clearing, satellite lesions, or irregular distribution — these suggest an alternative diagnosis
  • Bilateral involvement — true cellulitis is almost always unilateral; bilateral lower leg redness in a patient with known heart failure or venous disease is far more likely to be stasis dermatitis
  • Skin texture — tight, shiny, blistered, or darkened skin can indicate a more severe or necrotizing process

Marking the Border

One of the most useful things I do in the exam room — and one of the most practical things patients can do at home — is mark the border of the redness with a skin-safe marker or pen. Drawing a line around the leading edge, with the date and time noted, tells you whether the infection is spreading or shrinking when you check 12–24 hours later. If the redness is advancing beyond the line after 24–48 hours of antibiotics, that is a sign the treatment is not working and the patient needs re-evaluation.

When Imaging or Labs Are Needed

Most straightforward cellulitis cases need no additional workup. However, certain situations change that:

  • CT scan or MRI: Ordered when necrotizing fasciitis is suspected — look for severe pain disproportionate to skin appearance, skin discoloration, crepitus (air in tissue), or rapid systemic deterioration. Imaging helps identify gas in soft tissue planes, though a negative CT does not rule out necrotizing infection in early stages.
  • CBC and CMP: Reasonable for patients with fever, systemic symptoms, or those who are immunocompromised or diabetic. Elevated white cell count confirms inflammatory response. Elevated creatinine or glucose may affect antibiotic selection and disposition decisions.
  • Blood cultures: As noted above, reserved for patients with systemic signs or immunosuppression.
  • Ultrasound: Helpful when there is clinical uncertainty about whether a collection (abscess) is present beneath the cellulitis, particularly in obese patients where palpation is less reliable.

Cellulitis vs. Abscess vs. DVT

Getting the diagnosis right determines everything that follows. Three conditions are frequently confused with cellulitis, and each requires a different approach.

Feature Cellulitis Skin Abscess Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)
Primary problem Diffuse bacterial infection of dermis/subcutaneous tissue Localized collection of pus under the skin Blood clot in a deep vein, usually the leg
Skin appearance Diffuse redness with poorly defined, spreading borders Localized redness with central fluctuance or a visible head Normal skin or mild, blanchable redness; sometimes no redness at all
Skin temperature Warm or hot Warm, especially over the central collection Normal or mildly warm; rarely cool
Pus present No (if pus develops, an abscess has formed) Yes — the hallmark of abscess No
Bilateral? Almost always unilateral Almost always unilateral Usually unilateral, but bilateral DVT occurs
Fever common? In moderate-severe cases Can occur with larger abscesses Uncommon; if present, suggests concurrent pulmonary embolism
Primary treatment Antibiotics (oral or IV depending on severity) Incision and drainage; antibiotics for spreading cellulitis or high-risk patients Anticoagulation; NOT antibiotics
Diagnostic test Clinical; imaging if necrotizing infection suspected Clinical; point-of-care ultrasound to confirm collection Doppler ultrasound of the leg veins

The DVT distinction deserves particular emphasis. DVT can produce unilateral leg swelling, warmth, and tenderness — a presentation that closely mimics early cellulitis. The key differences are that DVT skin is usually not as brightly red, and the redness tends to be blanchable (the red color disappears when you press on it) rather than the fixed, non-blanchable erythema of cellulitis. Still, if there is diagnostic uncertainty in a patient with risk factors for clotting — recent surgery, prolonged immobility, malignancy, oral contraceptive use — a Doppler ultrasound is the right call.[5]

Stasis dermatitis is perhaps the most common misdiagnosis for bilateral lower extremity cellulitis. Stasis dermatitis is inflammation driven by chronic venous insufficiency — it produces redness, scaling, and itching in both legs, often in a classic "sock" distribution around the ankle and lower calf. It is not infectious and does not require antibiotics. Treating it as cellulitis delays the right treatment (compression, emollients, venous care) and exposes the patient to unnecessary antibiotics.

Treatment

Treatment decisions flow from two questions: Is this purulent (pus-forming) or non-purulent cellulitis? And are there risk factors for MRSA or signs of more severe infection? The IDSA 2014 skin and soft tissue infection guidelines provide a clear evidence-based framework for both.[1]

IDSA 2014 Classification: Mild, Moderate, Severe

Mild: No systemic signs of infection (no fever, normal heart rate, normal blood pressure). Appropriate for outpatient oral antibiotics.
Moderate: Systemic signs present (fever, elevated heart rate) but no organ dysfunction. May be managed outpatient with close follow-up, or with initial IV antibiotics followed by oral step-down.
Severe: Systemic signs plus evidence of deeper infection (bullae, skin sloughing, hypotension, organ dysfunction) or failure of outpatient treatment. Requires hospitalization and IV antibiotics.[1]

First-Line Oral Antibiotics: Non-Purulent Cellulitis (No MRSA Risk)

For typical, non-purulent cellulitis in a patient without MRSA risk factors, streptococcal coverage is the target. Beta-lactam antibiotics work well:

Antibiotic Typical Adult Regimen Notes
Cephalexin (Keflex) 500 mg four times daily × 5 days First-line per IDSA. Well-tolerated. Covers both Streptococcus and MSSA. Most commonly prescribed option in outpatient practice.
Dicloxacillin 500 mg four times daily × 5 days First-line per IDSA. Must be taken on an empty stomach. Excellent streptococcal and MSSA coverage. Less commonly prescribed than cephalexin but equivalent efficacy.
Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg twice daily × 5 days Reasonable second-line option with broader coverage, particularly for bite-associated cellulitis or when polymicrobial infection is suspected.
Clindamycin 300–450 mg three times daily × 5 days Alternative for penicillin-allergic patients. Check local susceptibility — clindamycin resistance among MRSA strains varies by region. Risk of C. difficile diarrhea.

The IDSA 2014 guidelines specify a 5-day course of antibiotics as the standard duration, extended if the infection has not clearly improved by that point.[1] The old reflexive 10-day course is no longer supported by evidence for uncomplicated, non-purulent cellulitis. Shorter courses reduce side effects, cost, and contribution to antibiotic resistance without reducing efficacy when the diagnosis is correct.

When MRSA Coverage Is Needed

MRSA should be considered in cellulitis when any of the following are present:

  • Prior documented MRSA infection
  • Penetrating trauma as the entry point
  • Known MRSA nasal colonization
  • Injection drug use
  • Systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) criteria met
  • Failure to respond to 48–72 hours of a beta-lactam antibiotic
  • Known community with high CA-MRSA prevalence
Antibiotic Typical Adult Regimen Notes
TMP-SMX (Bactrim DS) 1–2 DS tablets twice daily × 5–7 days Strong activity against CA-MRSA. Does not cover Group A Streptococcus adequately — consider adding cephalexin for empiric coverage of both. Contraindicated in sulfa allergy. Check renal function and potassium in patients on ACE inhibitors or ARBs.[4]
Doxycycline 100 mg twice daily × 5–7 days Effective against CA-MRSA; one study found a 96% success rate in cutaneous CA-MRSA infections treated with tetracyclines.[2] Take with food to reduce GI upset. Avoid in pregnancy. Good tolerability profile.
Clindamycin 300–450 mg three times daily × 5–7 days Option when susceptibility is confirmed. D-zone disk diffusion testing should be done to rule out inducible resistance before relying on clindamycin for MRSA.

For severe infections requiring hospitalization, intravenous vancomycin — or alternatively daptomycin or linezolid — provides MRSA coverage while awaiting culture results. Once the patient is clinically improving and tolerating oral intake, step-down to an appropriate oral agent is appropriate.[1]

Elevation and Supportive Care

Elevation of the affected limb above heart level reduces swelling and improves antibiotic delivery to the infected tissue by decreasing venous congestion. The IDSA guidelines specifically recommend elevation of the affected area along with treatment of any underlying predisposing conditions — both are given a "strong" recommendation.[1] Cold compresses or cool water may reduce discomfort, but heat should be avoided as it can increase bacterial proliferation.

The guidelines also note that systemic corticosteroids (such as prednisone 40 mg daily for 7 days) could be considered in non-diabetic adults with cellulitis, as a small number of studies suggest modestly faster symptom resolution. This is a weak recommendation and is not standard practice in most settings — the risk of steroid-related complications generally does not justify routine use.[1]

When to See a Doctor

Knowing where to go — and how fast — is one of the most practical decisions you face when you notice a red, swollen area on your skin. Here is the framework I use when talking with patients.

Scenario Appropriate Level of Care
Small area of redness and warmth, no fever, no systemic symptoms, you have mild risk factors (e.g., minor cut on leg) Telehealth visit: A physician can evaluate photos and your description, confirm the diagnosis, and prescribe the right antibiotic without you needing to leave home. Appropriate for mild, early-stage cellulitis in otherwise healthy patients.
Spreading redness that has grown over 24–48 hours, mild tenderness, low-grade fever under 100.4°F, manageable pain Urgent care or same-day physician visit: In-person evaluation allows border marking, assessment of depth, and closer examination for abscess or other complications. Prescriptions can be issued and a 24-hour follow-up plan established.
Fever above 101°F (38.3°C), rapidly spreading redness that has changed significantly in a few hours, significant pain, red streaking, or you are diabetic/immunocompromised with worsening symptoms Emergency department or urgent care with IV capability: May need IV antibiotics, labs, imaging, and closer monitoring. Do not wait overnight to see if it improves.
Redness involving the eye area (eyelid, around the orbit), any signs of vision change, pain with eye movement, or proptosis (eye appearing to bulge) Emergency department immediately: Periorbital and orbital cellulitis can threaten vision and spread to the brain. These require urgent CT scan and IV antibiotics, and often ophthalmology consultation.
Skin turning dark, purple, or black; excruciating pain out of proportion to appearance; gas or crackling felt under the skin; signs of sepsis (confusion, extremely rapid heart rate, low blood pressure) Call 911 or go to the ER by the fastest means available. These are signs of necrotizing fasciitis or sepsis — both life-threatening emergencies requiring immediate surgical evaluation.

What I tell patients is this: a telehealth visit is genuinely appropriate for many cellulitis presentations. You do not automatically need an emergency room. But if there is any doubt — if the redness is moving fast, if you feel systemically unwell, or if you are in a high-risk group — go in person and go quickly.

Red Flags

Seek Emergency Care Immediately For:
  • Rapidly spreading redness — borders that are visibly advancing over a period of hours, not days
  • Fever above 101°F (38.3°C) — signals systemic infection that may require IV antibiotics
  • Severe pain disproportionate to the skin appearance — the hallmark warning sign for necrotizing fasciitis, where deep tissue destruction outpaces what you can see on the surface
  • Skin blistering, darkening, or sloughing — indicates a severe infectious process with tissue death
  • Periorbital or orbital involvement — any cellulitis involving the eye area requires emergency evaluation because orbital cellulitis can cause blindness and meningitis if not treated with IV antibiotics promptly
  • Crepitus (crackling sensation under the skin) — indicates gas-producing bacteria and necrotizing infection
  • Signs of sepsis — confusion, rapid heart rate, low blood pressure, or extreme fatigue alongside skin infection
  • Immunocompromised patients — anyone on chemotherapy, biologic therapy, corticosteroids, or with HIV, organ transplant, or poorly controlled diabetes should be evaluated in person for any but the most minor skin infections

Necrotizing fasciitis — sometimes called "flesh-eating bacteria" in the media — is rare but worth understanding because the window for effective surgical intervention is narrow. The bacteria involved (most often Group A Streptococcus, Clostridium species, or mixed organisms) produce enzymes that destroy the fascia and subcutaneous tissue faster than the skin surface shows. Pain that seems completely out of proportion to what the skin looks like is the most important clinical clue. If you or someone else describes the pain as "the worst ever" and the redness seems almost unremarkable in comparison, that is an emergency.

Prevention

Cellulitis recurrence rates are high — studies estimate 8–20% of patients will have another episode within a year, and once you have had two or three infections, the pattern tends to continue unless the underlying factors are actively managed.[3] Prevention is where the most meaningful long-term gains are made.

Treat Tinea Pedis First

Every patient I see with recurrent lower extremity cellulitis gets a careful foot exam, and many have tinea pedis they were either unaware of or not treating consistently. Interdigital tinea pedis — the classic "athlete's foot" between the toes — creates chronic skin fissuring that is the most common bacterial entry point for cellulitis in the lower leg. Topical terbinafine or clotrimazole applied once daily for one week has high cure rates for interdigital tinea. Systemic antifungal therapy (oral fluconazole or itraconazole) is used for resistant or widespread infection.[3]

Compression for Lymphedema and Venous Insufficiency

Chronic leg swelling dramatically increases cellulitis risk by creating a stagnant, protein-rich tissue environment that bacteria thrive in. A randomized trial found that compression stocking therapy (providing at least 23 mmHg of pressure) in patients with chronic leg edema and prior cellulitis reduced recurrence from 40% in controls to 15% in the compression group — a number needed to treat of 4.[5] Over-the-counter compression stockings at 20–30 mmHg are available at most pharmacies. Compression should generally be started after the acute cellulitis has resolved.

Skin Care and Barrier Maintenance

Dry, cracked skin — particularly on the feet and lower legs — loses its protective barrier function. Regular application of emollients (unscented moisturizers) to the lower extremities reduces fissuring and helps maintain intact skin. Gentle daily washing, particularly between the toes, removes bacteria before they can establish themselves. Patients with venous stasis dermatitis should have this treated actively, since inflamed skin is far more permeable than healthy skin.

Managing Systemic Risk Factors

Blood glucose control in diabetics reduces both the frequency and severity of skin infections. Weight management, though difficult, reduces the lymphatic and venous stasis that drives lower extremity cellulitis. Patients with lymphedema benefit from working with a lymphedema therapist to optimize drainage technique and compression wrapping.

Antibiotic Prophylaxis for Recurrent Cases

For patients who continue to have 3–4 episodes per year despite addressing modifiable risk factors, the IDSA 2014 guidelines support prophylactic antibiotics — oral penicillin or erythromycin twice daily for 4–52 weeks, or intramuscular benzathine penicillin every 2–4 weeks.[1] A 2013 UK trial (the PATCH trial) confirmed that low-dose penicillin prophylaxis reduced recurrence by about 45% during the treatment period. This is viewed as a later-line option — a bridge while underlying conditions are treated — rather than a substitute for addressing root causes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Cellulitis does not reliably clear on its own. Unlike some superficial skin irritations, cellulitis is a bacterial infection of the deeper skin layers that requires antibiotic treatment. Without antibiotics, the infection is likely to spread and may progress to bacteremia or sepsis. Early treatment with the right antibiotic typically produces clear improvement within 24–48 hours. Do not wait more than 1–2 days if you suspect cellulitis.

The clearest sign of worsening cellulitis is redness that spreads beyond the border you marked at the start of treatment. Also watch for increasing pain or swelling, development of fever or chills, skin that begins to blister or change color (darker, purple, or mottled), or red streaking extending from the affected area. If any of these occur after starting antibiotics, contact your physician the same day. Fever above 101°F (38.3°C) or rapid spread of redness are reasons to go to the emergency department without waiting.

Cellulitis itself is not contagious from person to person. It develops when bacteria enter your own skin through a break or compromised barrier — not through casual contact with another person who has cellulitis. That said, MRSA bacteria can colonize skin surfaces and spread between people through direct contact, so good hand hygiene around open wounds and avoiding sharing towels or personal items with someone who has an active MRSA infection is sensible practice.

An abscess is a localized collection of pus under the skin — you feel it as a tender, soft, fluctuant lump that may have a visible white or yellow center. Cellulitis is a diffuse infection of the tissue itself, without a walled-off pus pocket. The distinction matters for treatment: abscesses primarily need drainage (incision and drainage), while cellulitis is treated with antibiotics. Both can coexist — an abscess surrounded by spreading redness gets both drainage and antibiotics. If you feel a soft, movable center in the middle of the red area, tell your physician; that changes the plan.

Most cellulitis cases do not require an ER visit. A small, early-stage infection without fever in an otherwise healthy person can be evaluated and treated through a telehealth visit or urgent care appointment. Go to the emergency department if you have a fever above 101°F (38.3°C), rapidly expanding redness that is visibly growing over hours, pain out of proportion to the skin changes, skin blistering or darkening, or any cellulitis involving the eye area. Immunocompromised patients and those with poorly controlled diabetes should have a lower threshold for emergency evaluation.

Recurrent cellulitis almost always reflects persistent underlying risk factors rather than antibiotic failure. The most frequent culprits are untreated or undertreated tinea pedis (athlete's foot), chronic leg swelling from lymphedema or venous insufficiency, obesity, and poorly controlled diabetes. Each episode of cellulitis can also damage lymphatic channels, making future episodes more likely. The most effective prevention strategy is to identify and address the specific underlying factors in your case — not simply to repeat antibiotics every time it recurs. For patients with 3–4 episodes per year despite managing risk factors, the IDSA guidelines support prophylactic low-dose antibiotics.[1]
