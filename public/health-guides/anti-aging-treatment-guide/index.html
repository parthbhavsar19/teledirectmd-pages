Anti-Aging Skin Treatment Guide

Retinoids, sunscreen, and evidence-based ingredients — what dermatology actually supports

TeleDirectMD Medical Team
Last reviewed: Mar 2026
~15 min read

Key Points

  • Sunscreen is the single most effective anti-aging intervention, supported by decades of clinical evidence.
  • Prescription tretinoin (0.025–0.1%) has more randomized controlled trial data for reversing photoaging than any other topical agent.
  • Retinoid dermatitis is manageable — start low, go slow, and use the buffering method to improve tolerance.
  • Vitamin C (L-ascorbic acid), niacinamide, and alpha hydroxy acids each have solid clinical evidence for specific aging concerns.
  • Glogau classification helps determine whether topical treatments, chemical peels, or laser procedures are most appropriate.
  • Many marketed anti-aging ingredients lack meaningful clinical data — understanding what the evidence actually shows protects your time and money.
  • Tretinoin and other prescription retinoids are accessible through a telehealth visit, without an in-person dermatologist appointment.

Photoaging vs. Chronological Aging: Why the Distinction Matters

Skin ages through two distinct processes, and treatment depends on which one is driving the changes you see.

Chronological aging is time-dependent and largely genetic. It produces thin, dry skin with fine lines, decreased elasticity, and reduced collagen production — but relatively little pigment irregularity. You see this in skin that has been consistently protected from the sun.

Photoaging — technically called dermatoheliosis — is UV-driven and superimposed on chronological aging. It accounts for an estimated 80–90% of the visible changes most people associate with skin aging: deep wrinkles, mottled hyperpigmentation, solar lentigines (age spots), telangiectasia, sallowness, leathery texture, and skin laxity. The mechanism is well established — UV radiation increases the production of matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs), which degrade collagen and elastin, while simultaneously generating reactive oxygen species that damage DNA and disrupt normal pigmentation.[7]

UVA rays (320–400 nm) penetrate deeply into the dermis, causing oxidative damage and long-term collagen breakdown. UVB rays (280–320 nm) act more superficially but are more potent at inducing direct DNA damage and epidermal changes. Both contribute to photoaging. Both are blocked by properly applied broad-spectrum sunscreen.

Clinical Insight

In my experience treating patients via telemedicine, the most striking conversations are with patients in their 50s who have used sunscreen consistently since their 30s. Their skin tells a different story than peers who didn't. UV prevention compounds over decades — you cannot undo decades of UV damage in six months, but you can absolutely improve it.

The Glogau Scale: Classifying Photoaging Severity

The Glogau Photoaging Classification — developed by dermatologist Richard Glogau — is the standard clinical tool for grading photoaging severity. It guides treatment selection.

Glogau Type Typical Age Clinical Features First-Line Approach
Type I — "No Wrinkles" 20s–30s Early photoaging; minimal pigmentation changes; no keratoses; skin tone generally even Daily sunscreen, antioxidant serum, begin low-dose retinoid
Type II — "Wrinkles in Motion" 30s–40s Wrinkles appear with facial movement; early lentigines; pores more visible; no keratoses at rest Tretinoin, sunscreen, vitamin C, AHA exfoliant; consider superficial chemical peel
Type III — "Wrinkles at Rest" 50s Static wrinkles present; prominent brown spots; telangiectasia; visible actinic keratoses Prescription retinoid + procedural combination (medium peel, microneedling, or laser)
Type IV — "Only Wrinkles" 60s–70s Wrinkles across entire face at rest; yellow-gray skin tone; prior skin cancers or actinic lesions CO2 fractional laser resurfacing; ablative procedures; oncologic surveillance

Most patients seen in a primary care or telemedicine setting fall into Glogau Types I and II, where topical treatments produce meaningful results. Types III and IV typically benefit from combining topical therapies with dermatologic procedures.

Sunscreen: The #1 Anti-Aging Intervention

No other anti-aging product has the evidence base of daily sunscreen use. The American Academy of Dermatology recommends a broad-spectrum sunscreen with SPF 30 or higher, applied every day — including cloudy days, winter months, and days spent mostly indoors near windows.[2]

Here is what the labels mean:

  • Broad-spectrum: Protects against both UVA (aging) and UVB (burning and DNA damage) radiation.
  • SPF 30: Blocks approximately 97% of UVB rays when applied correctly. SPF 50 blocks about 98%. Higher SPF numbers offer diminishing returns — correct application matters more than chasing a higher number.
  • Water resistance: Maintains efficacy for 40 or 80 minutes of water exposure. Reapply every 2 hours outdoors regardless.

Sunscreen chemistry falls into two main categories. Chemical sunscreens (avobenzone, octinoxate, oxybenzone) absorb UV radiation and convert it to heat. Mineral sunscreens (zinc oxide, titanium dioxide) physically reflect UV rays. Both are effective. Mineral formulations tend to be better tolerated in patients with sensitive skin or acne-prone skin.

What I Tell Patients

The best sunscreen is the one you will actually use every day. If a formula feels greasy, you will skip it. Try tinted mineral formulations, lightweight chemical hybrids, or SPF-containing moisturizers until you find one that fits your daily routine. Consistency matters more than the exact formulation.

Topical Retinoids: Tretinoin, Adapalene, and Tazarotene

Retinoids are vitamin A derivatives and represent the most thoroughly studied topical agents for photoaging. Among them, tretinoin (all-trans retinoic acid) has the deepest evidence base — decades of randomized controlled trials showing consistent improvement in wrinkling, hyperpigmentation, sallowness, and lentigines.[1]

How Retinoids Work

Tretinoin binds directly to retinoic acid receptors (RARs) in skin cells, triggering transcription of genes that regulate keratinocyte proliferation, epidermal thickening, collagen synthesis, and MMP suppression. The result is accelerated cell turnover, compaction of the stratum corneum, increased epidermal thickness, and new collagen deposition in the dermis. UVB-damaged skin shows measurable structural improvements within 3 to 4 months of consistent use, with continued benefit through 24 months.

Prescription Retinoids: Tretinoin

Tretinoin is FDA-approved for treating photodamaged skin and requires a prescription in the United States. Available concentrations range from 0.02% to 0.1%:

  • 0.025%: Starting concentration for most patients; produces significant improvement with a lower irritation profile.
  • 0.05%: The most studied strength; the reference concentration in the majority of clinical trials.
  • 0.1%: Highest concentration; faster results but substantially more irritation — reserved for patients who have already adapted to lower doses.

A systematic review of seven randomized controlled trials found that topical tretinoin across these concentrations consistently improved clinical signs of photoaging, with improvement apparent as early as one month and maintained at 24 months of follow-up.[1] Tactile roughness was the one parameter that did not consistently improve — everything else did.

Other Prescription Options

Tazarotene (0.05%, 0.1%) is a synthetic retinoid with a slightly different receptor binding profile. Clinical trials comparing tazarotene 0.1% to tretinoin 0.05% found that tazarotene produces faster initial improvement, though results equalize with continued use. It also carries greater irritation potential.

Adapalene (0.3%) — available as both prescription and OTC at lower concentrations — was developed primarily for acne but shows meaningful anti-aging activity comparable to lower-dose tretinoin. It tends to be better tolerated and is a reasonable option for patients who are highly sensitive to tretinoin.

OTC Retinoids: Retinol and Retinaldehyde

Retinol is the most widely used cosmeceutical retinoid. It requires enzymatic conversion by the skin to retinaldehyde, then to retinoic acid — making it approximately 10 times less potent than tretinoin. Results take longer to appear and tend to be less dramatic, but tolerability is significantly better. If you cannot access a prescription or need to build tolerance before starting prescription-strength tretinoin, 0.3–0.5% retinol is a clinically reasonable starting point.

Retinoid Type Strength Range Evidence Level Irritation
Tretinoin Prescription 0.025%–0.1% High (multiple RCTs) Moderate–High initially
Tazarotene Prescription 0.05%–0.1% High High
Adapalene 0.3% Prescription / OTC 0.1% 0.1%–0.3% Moderate–High Low–Moderate
Retinol OTC 0.1%–1% Moderate Low–Moderate

Managing Retinoid Dermatitis

Retinoid dermatitis — redness, peeling, burning, and tightness — is the primary reason patients abandon treatment. It is expected in the first 4 to 8 weeks and typically resolves as the skin adapts. Understanding why it happens and how to manage it makes the difference between patients who succeed and those who give up early.

Retinoids thin the stratum corneum and accelerate cell turnover, which temporarily reduces the skin's barrier function and increases transepidermal water loss. The result is irritation that feels worse than the underlying skin problem.[8]

The Start-Low, Go-Slow Protocol

  1. Begin at the lowest effective concentration — 0.025% tretinoin for most new users.
  2. Apply every 2 to 3 nights initially, not every night. Increase frequency after 4–6 weeks if tolerating well.
  3. Use the sandwich (buffer) method: apply a moisturizer first, wait 10–15 minutes, apply a pea-sized amount of tretinoin, then apply moisturizer again on top. This slows absorption and reduces immediate irritation.
  4. Avoid mixing retinoids with exfoliants (AHAs, BHAs) or high-concentration vitamin C in the same application — especially in the first 3 months.
  5. Skip the under-eye area and corners of the nose and mouth unless directed otherwise; these areas are more sensitive.
  6. If significant peeling or burning occurs, reduce to 2 nights per week and rebuild frequency slowly. Do not stop entirely unless a rash or blistering develops.
Important

Tretinoin and all retinoids are contraindicated in pregnancy due to the teratogenic risk established with oral retinoids. Women of childbearing age should use reliable contraception and discuss this explicitly with their prescriber. Retinoids should also be avoided while breastfeeding.

Evidence-Based Topical Actives Beyond Retinoids

Vitamin C (L-Ascorbic Acid)

Vitamin C is one of the most studied antioxidants in skincare. The active form for skin use is L-ascorbic acid, which must be present at adequate concentration (typically 10–20%) and a low pH (around 3.5) to penetrate the skin effectively.

Its anti-aging actions are several: it neutralizes free radicals generated by UV exposure, acts as a cofactor for collagen-synthesizing enzymes (prolyl and lysyl hydroxylase), stabilizes collagen fibers, inhibits melanin formation by blocking tyrosinase, and provides supplementary photoprotection when used under sunscreen.[4]

Clinical trials support these effects. A double-blind placebo-controlled study using 5% topical vitamin C over six months showed significant reduction in deep wrinkles and improved skin texture. Another study using 10% L-ascorbic acid for 12 weeks demonstrated statistically significant reduction in photoaging scores compared to placebo.[4]

Apply vitamin C in the morning before sunscreen. Formulation stability is a real issue — L-ascorbic acid oxidizes when exposed to air and light. Choose products in opaque, air-limiting packaging and replace after opening according to the manufacturer's guidance.

Niacinamide

Niacinamide (vitamin B3) has become one of the most clinically versatile topical ingredients. At concentrations of 4–5%, it improves fine lines and wrinkles, reduces hyperpigmented spots, decreases redness and blotchiness, and strengthens the skin barrier by increasing ceramide and collagen synthesis.

What I find particularly useful is niacinamide's tolerability profile. It rarely causes irritation, works well at a broad pH range, and can be combined with most other active ingredients — including retinoids. For patients who are managing retinoid dermatitis, adding niacinamide to their moisturizer often provides meaningful relief. Evidence level for niacinamide is high, with several peer-reviewed randomized studies supporting its effects on hyperpigmentation and barrier function.[5]

Alpha Hydroxy Acids (Glycolic Acid, Lactic Acid)

Alpha hydroxy acids (AHAs) work by disrupting the bonds between corneocytes in the outermost skin layer, promoting exfoliation and accelerating cellular turnover. Glycolic acid — derived from sugarcane — has the smallest molecular weight and deepest penetration of the AHAs, making it the most studied.

At concentrations of 5–10% for daily use, AHAs reduce fine lines, improve skin texture, decrease hyperpigmentation, and increase dermal hyaluronic acid and collagen gene expression.[5] Lactic acid behaves similarly but has an additional moisturizing effect, making it better suited for patients with dry or sensitive skin.

AHAs also serve as a bridge for patients not yet ready for a prescription retinoid. They provide visible improvement in texture and brightness with a less intense initial experience than tretinoin.

Hyaluronic Acid

Hyaluronic acid is a glycosaminoglycan naturally found in the dermis that holds up to 1,000 times its weight in water. Production declines with age, contributing to loss of skin volume and hydration. Topical hyaluronic acid serums work primarily at the surface — their large molecular weight limits dermal penetration — but they effectively attract moisture to the skin surface, plumping fine lines temporarily and improving overall hydration and barrier resilience.[3]

This makes hyaluronic acid an excellent addition to a retinoid-containing routine. Applying it before or after tretinoin reduces transepidermal water loss and minimizes dryness without interfering with retinoid efficacy.

Peptides

Peptides are short amino acid sequences that can signal skin cells to produce more collagen or inhibit enzymes that break down collagen. Several classes have been studied: signal peptides (e.g., palmitoyl tripeptide-38, Matrixyl), carrier peptides, and neurotransmitter-inhibiting peptides (marketed as topical Botox alternatives).

The evidence is mixed but growing. A clinical study on a triple peptide complex (3%) found improvement in photodamaged skin within one month. Multiple studies on palmitoyl pentapeptide-4 showed visible wrinkle reduction. That said, the evidence base is considerably smaller than for retinoids, and most studies have been funded by manufacturers. Peptides are well tolerated and a reasonable addition for patients seeking retinoid-free options or looking to add to an established routine — but I would not position them as replacements for tretinoin.

Ingredients That Lack Clinical Support

The skincare market runs ahead of the science. Many products sold as anti-aging treatments rely on compelling mechanisms or single in-vitro studies rather than clinical data. Being able to distinguish the evidence-supported from the speculative saves patients time and money.

  • Topical collagen creams: Collagen molecules are too large (300,000+ daltons) to penetrate the dermis through intact skin. These products do not replace dermal collagen — they may provide surface hydration, but that is a moisturizer effect, not a collagen effect.
  • Stem cell extracts: Most are derived from plants or apple cells. There is no credible evidence that plant stem cells meaningfully influence human skin cell behavior when applied topically.
  • Placenta-derived compounds: Used in some luxury products. No peer-reviewed clinical trials demonstrate meaningful anti-aging effects in human skin.
  • EGF (epidermal growth factor) serums: The topical bioavailability and stability of EGF through intact skin is not well established in independent studies. The theoretical mechanism is interesting; the clinical evidence is weak.
  • Gold and caviar: No evidence base beyond marketing. These are prestige ingredients in expensive products.
  • Most single-antioxidant serums (other than vitamin C): Resveratrol, coenzyme Q10, and many others show interesting in-vitro findings but lack the well-documented human RCT evidence that vitamin C has accumulated.

Procedural Treatments: Chemical Peels, Microneedling, and Laser Resurfacing

When topical treatments are insufficient for the degree of photoaging, or when a patient wants faster and more significant results, procedural options become relevant. These are typically handled by dermatologists or licensed proceduralists — but understanding them helps you have an informed conversation about what is appropriate for your skin.

Chemical Peels

Chemical peels use acidic agents to remove damaged outer skin layers and stimulate regeneration. Classification by depth:

  • Superficial peels (glycolic acid 20–70%, lactic acid, salicylic acid 10–30%): Target the epidermis. Appropriate for Glogau Type I–II. Minimal downtime (1–3 days of redness/mild peeling). Multiple sessions needed.
  • Medium peels (trichloroacetic acid 15–35%, Jessner's solution + TCA): Reach the papillary dermis. Appropriate for Glogau Type II–III. Downtime 5–7 days. Clinical studies show significant improvement in skin elasticity, hydration, melanin distribution, and wrinkle depth.
  • Deep peels (phenol, high-concentration TCA): Reach the reticular dermis. Most dramatic results but longest recovery (weeks) and higher risk of scarring and pigment changes. Requires careful patient selection.

A 2019 study comparing glycolic acid/TCA combination peels with 35% TCA alone found both significantly improved photoaging parameters, with the combination showing better hydration and melanin reduction.[6] The best outcomes are achieved when chemical peels are combined with a pre-treatment retinoid regimen of 4–6 weeks.

Microneedling (Percutaneous Collagen Induction Therapy)

Microneedling creates controlled micro-injuries in the skin using fine needles (0.5–2.5 mm in length), triggering the wound healing cascade — platelet activation, growth factor release, fibroblast stimulation, and new collagen and elastin synthesis. Studies have shown collagen and elastin levels in treated skin can increase by approximately 400% after six months, with stratum granulosum thickening observed at one year.[9]

Microneedling is effective for fine lines, skin texture, pore size, and mild-to-moderate photoaging (Glogau II–III). It carries a lower risk of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation than ablative lasers, making it suitable for a broader range of skin tones. Results are enhanced when combined with topical vitamin C or tretinoin. Allow 2–4 weeks between sessions for optimal healing and collagen remodeling.

Laser Resurfacing

Laser resurfacing uses focused light energy to remove damaged epidermal and superficial dermal layers while inducing thermal collagen contraction. Two primary categories:

  • Ablative lasers (CO2, Erbium:YAG): Remove skin layers directly. CO2 laser resurfacing has well-documented clinical evidence for reducing photoaging, rhytids, and skin laxity through collagen remodeling. Fractional delivery systems (where the laser treats a grid of micro-columns rather than the full surface) have significantly reduced recovery time and risk while preserving substantial effectiveness.[10] Full-ablative CO2 resurfacing recovery is approximately two weeks; fractional CO2 typically requires 5–7 days.
  • Non-ablative lasers (1064 nm Nd:YAG, pulsed dye laser, IPL): Stimulate collagen without removing the surface. Results are more gradual and subtle, but downtime is minimal. Better suited for Glogau Type I–II or as maintenance after ablative procedures.

Laser resurfacing is best for Glogau Type III–IV photoaging. It offers more precise control than dermabrasion or chemical peels. The trade-off is higher cost, greater technical skill requirements, and a non-trivial risk of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation in darker skin tones — which demands careful patient selection and pre-treatment.

Prescription Retinoids and Telehealth Access

Tretinoin and higher-strength tazarotene require a prescription in the United States. For years, that meant a dermatologist appointment — which could mean a 3–6 month wait in many parts of the country. Telehealth has changed that.

A licensed physician can evaluate your skin concerns, review your medical history, and prescribe tretinoin during a video visit. This typically takes 20–30 minutes and eliminates the geographic barrier that leaves many patients relying on OTC retinols when prescription-strength tretinoin would be more appropriate for their degree of photodamage.

What to expect in a telemedicine consultation for anti-aging:

  • Review of your current skincare routine and any previous retinoid use
  • Discussion of your skin type, sensitivity, and specific concerns (pigmentation, texture, fine lines)
  • Assessment of photoaging severity to match the right starting concentration
  • A written regimen covering the full stack: sunscreen, vitamin C timing, retinoid protocol, and management plan for the initial adaptation period
  • Follow-up guidance on when and how to increase concentration

Patients with acne, rosacea, or significant post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation benefit especially from a physician-guided approach, since these conditions influence retinoid tolerance and require a more personalized treatment sequence.

Here is how the evidence-supported ingredients work together in practice:
